Process: Receptor Tyrosine Kinase Signaling

The six proteins encoded by the genes ERBB2, ERBB4, FGFR2, FGFR4, HRAS, and KRAS are all integral components of the receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) signaling pathway, which is crucial for cell growth, differentiation, and survival.

ERBB2 and ERBB4 are members of the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) family of RTKs. They are activated by ligand binding, leading to receptor dimerization and autophosphorylation. This initiates a cascade of downstream signaling events. ERBB2 is unique in that it has no known ligands, and it prefers to form heterodimers with other EGFR family members, enhancing their kinase activity.

FGFR2 and FGFR4 are part of the fibroblast growth factor receptor (FGFR) family of RTKs. They are activated by fibroblast growth factors, leading to receptor dimerization and autophosphorylation. This triggers downstream signaling pathways that regulate cellular processes such as proliferation, differentiation, and migration.

HRAS and KRAS are GTPases that act as molecular switches in RTK signaling. They are activated by guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) that catalyze the exchange of GDP for GTP. Once activated, RAS proteins can interact with a variety of effector proteins to propagate the signal downstream.

The interaction between these proteins forms a complex network of signaling events that regulate key cellular processes. Dysregulation of this system, such as mutations that lead to constitutive activation of RTKs or RAS proteins, can result in uncontrolled cell growth and cancer. Therefore, understanding the precise mechanisms of RTK signaling and its regulation is crucial for the development of targeted cancer therapies.

XPC is involved in damage recognition, while XPA verifies the presence of damage and recruits other proteins to the site of damage.

Process: DNA Mismatch Repair

The system of interacting proteins encoded by the human genes MLH1, MSH2, MSH3, MSH6, and PMS2 primarily performs the biological process of DNA mismatch repair (MMR). 

The MMR system is crucial for maintaining genomic stability. It corrects base-base mismatches and insertion/deletion loops (IDLs) that occur during DNA replication and recombination, thereby preventing mutations from becoming permanent in dividing cells. 

MSH2, MSH3, and MSH6 form heterodimers that recognize and bind to mismatches. MSH2-MSH6 (MutSα) primarily recognizes base-base mismatches and small IDLs, while MSH2-MSH3 (MutSβ) binds to larger IDLs. The binding of these heterodimers to the mismatch initiates the repair process.

MLH1 and PMS2 form a heterodimer (MutLα) that interacts with the mismatch-bound MutSα or MutSβ. This interaction triggers the excision of the mismatched segment, followed by resynthesis using the original DNA strand as a template.

The importance of this process is underscored by the fact that defects in MMR genes, including MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2, are associated with Lynch syndrome, a hereditary condition that significantly increases the risk of various types of cancer, particularly colorectal cancer. This association provides strong evidence for the critical role of these proteins in DNA mismatch repair and maintaining genomic stability.
